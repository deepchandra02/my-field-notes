For a long time I treated writing as something you did only when you had something to say. The result was that I almost never wrote, because I almost never had a complete thought to defend.

## Drafts in public

The shift came when I started treating posts as drafts rather than artifacts. A note doesn't need to be finished. It needs to be *useful enough to come back to*.

<Aside>
  This is also why I separate essays from notes here. Notes are for unfinished thinking. Essays are when a note has earned a longer form.
</Aside>

Most of what I publish I would rewrite a year later. That's fine. The point isn't the post — the point is the practice.

## What public adds

There's a particular discipline that arrives the moment you know someone might read a thing. You stop using shorthand only you understand. You stop hand-waving past the hard parts. You don't get away with the *obviously, X* sentence anymore.

That friction is the whole point. The audience doesn't have to be large. It just has to be *imagined*.

## What public takes away

The temptation, of course, is to perform. To write the post you think will be read rather than the one you need to think through. The cost of writing in public is that this temptation never goes away — you just learn to notice it sooner.

## What's left

A working notebook. Half-formed observations alongside finished pieces. The mix is the point. If everything looked polished, you'd suspect (correctly) that most of the actual thinking was happening somewhere else.
